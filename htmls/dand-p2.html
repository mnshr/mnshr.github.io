Introduction:¶

The sinking of the RMS Titanic is one of the most infamous shipwrecks in history. On April 15, 1912, during her maiden voyage, the Titanic sank after colliding with an iceberg, killing 1502 out of 2224 passengers and crew. This sensational tragedy shocked the international community and led to better safety regulations for ships.

In this data analysis exercise, I try to perform a typical data analysis starting with data wrangling, performing exploratory analysis and a coming to a conclusion to answer a set of pre-determined questions. For data wrangling, I would examine the overall dataset using standard statistical measures such as the number of features, their types (whether numeric or categorical) and metrics such as averages, medians, quantiles, min/max values etc. I would also check a crossection of data to take a look at the values of different features. I would then try to fill the NULLs using an intuitive value, categorize variables into smaller number of groups if required. For data analysis I would try to answer a few fundamental questions, in this case the survival rate of passengers and the factors affecting that. I would analyze gender, age, family sizes, passenger classes and fare spent and try to answer the question.

When investigating the raw data we find that there are 171 missing age records out of total 891 records. The Pandas data frame has substituted a NaN for missing age values. We will have to update these NaNs to an appropriate value to proceed. There are a few options to replace the NaNs:

* Average of all ages
* Median of all ages
* Fill random values based on the provided ages
* Mean or Median of particular group to which the person with a NaN belongs

Since Median is more robust measure of centrality, I'll consider median as the best option. We can create 6 groups based on Passenger Class and Gender (3 * 2), and assign the median of these 6 groups to the NaNs belonging to respective groups. Now to convert NaNs for the Age variable, I will create an array called median_ages and fill the medians based on Gender and PClass variables as shown below. Next I'll create an additional column and fill the NaNs values.

In [54]:

4.2.1 Statistical Test¶

I performed the Chi-Square statistical test to verify the survival rate differences based on Gender: H0 - The survival rates of population means of Females is same as that of Males OR

      f = m

Ha - The survival rate of population means of Females and Males are different

      f  m

where f is the population mean of female survived and m is the population mean of male survived.

4.3.1 Statistical Test¶

I divided the Familiy variable (which has 9 categories based on family size) into 3 groups as explained below and performed the Chi-Square test:

H0 - The population mean of survived passengers among all family groups is same

      s = sf = lf

Ha - The population mean of survived passengers among at least 2 family groups is not same

      s  sf  lf

where :

  s is the population mean of single members who survived and 

  sf is the population mean of families which are small ranging from 2 to 4 members and

  lf is the population mean of families of size greater than 4.

4.5.1 Statsitical Test¶
Fare Group¶

Based on the 4 Fare Groups shown above in the bar chart, I conduct the following hypothesis test :

H0 - The population mean of survived passengers among Fare 4 groups is same and Survival is independent of Fare

l = m = h = vh

Ha - The popluation mean of survived passengers among Fare 4 groups are different and Surivival is dependent on Fare

l  m  h  vh

where:

l, m, h, vh are population means for Low Fare, Medium Fare, High Fare and Very High Fare respectively.

5: Conclusion¶

Based on the analysis, the survival rate for Women and Children is found to be high. Same can be said for the families of medium size. The survival rate was also high for higher paying class 1 passengers. I have conducted statistical analysis to verify these findings although these relations don't lead to causation.

Although since there are many missing aspects that could contribute to the accuracy of this analysis. As we know that sinking of the Titanic killed 1502 out of 2224 passengers and crew, while this data set has only 891 values. So we are working with only 40% of the data. Any inferences from this limited data have to be digested with an understanding that we are not seeing the complete picture but just a part of it. Another exmaple is the case of Age variable as there were more than 170 missing values, recorded as NaNs which is almost 20% of the entire dataset. Probably having those values could lead to more robust conclusions when it comes to survival analysis by age. A feature that could have helped the investigation better would be the location of different people in the ship. The variable Cabin could be of help if each cabin could be mapped and distance from the nearest escape point could be determined and used for our analysis. This could help us understand how far they were from available help and while the ship was sinking.

Data¶

VARIABLE DESCRIPTIONS: survival Survival (0 = No; 1 = Yes) pclass Passenger Class (1 = 1st; 2 = 2nd; 3 = 3rd) name Name sex Sex age Age sibsp Number of Siblings/Spouses Aboard parch Number of Parents/Children Aboard ticket Ticket Number fare Passenger Fare cabin Cabin embarked Port of Embarkation (C = Cherbourg; Q = Queenstown; S = Southampton)

SPECIAL NOTES: Pclass is a proxy for socio-economic status (SES) 1st ~ Upper; 2nd ~ Middle; 3rd ~ Lower

Age is in Years; Fractional if Age less than One (1) If the Age is Estimated, it is in the form xx.5

With respect to the family relation variables (i.e. sibsp and parch) some relations were ignored. The following are the definitions used for sibsp and parch.

Sibling: Brother, Sister, Stepbrother, or Stepsister of Passenger Aboard Titanic Spouse: Husband or Wife of Passenger Aboard Titanic (Mistresses and Fiances Ignored) Parent: Mother or Father of Passenger Aboard Titanic Child: Son, Daughter, Stepson, or Stepdaughter of Passenger Aboard Titanic

Other family relatives excluded from this study include cousins, nephews/nieces, aunts/uncles, and in-laws. Some children travelled only with a nanny, therefore parch=0 for them. As well, some travelled with very close friends or neighbors in a village, however, the definitions do not support such relations.
